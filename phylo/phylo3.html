Phylogeny highlights evolutionary relationships inferred from morphological and molecular data. (1.3)

Phylogeny is the study of an organism and its evolutionary relationships with other organisms. A circular tree of life was displayed in the last sub-section, using something called a phylogenetic tree. A phylogenetic tree is a tree that shows the evolutionary relationship between various species.

How do phylogenetic trees reflect evolutionary relationships?

A phylogenetic tree is a tree that shows the evolutionary relationship between various species. Below is an example of a phylogenetic tree. Try to see what you can infer from the picture.

You would notice these involve a sequence of organisms leading to the last organism in the tree, from one organism to the next, and so on. The organisms at the end are what we would call nodes.

The branch points (intersection between each line) describe a speciation event, where a new species becomes established by a change from its parents. Simply, this means that a lineage diverges and separates.

How do scientists make a phylogenetic tree?

This is where the title of this section becomes relevant! Phylogeny (and in turn phylogenetic trees) infer from morphological and molecular data. Let's start with the first one.

By morphological, we mean referring to the form or structure of things . This is manifested in biology as the things we call characters. Let's go back to the phylogenetic tree we saw earlier.

As you saw, before the branch points, red points represent the character transferred down the phylogenetic tree. These are what we call character states. Each character determines speciation, and we can see that it diverges the lineages into two. But you can also use the phylogenetic tree to determine the traits a creature can have too.

Let's take, for example, say, a lizard. A lizard has jaws, lungs and four limps, claws or nails, and keratinous scales. On the next page, we will see how molecular data shapes a phylogenetic tree. Do you suppose molecular data would change a morphological tree a lot?
